How the Great Lakes region became America's unexpected data center hub—and what 50 billion gallons of water have to do with it

The New Gold Rush

In 2019, the Great Lakes region hosted 12 major data centers. Today, that number has tripled. By 2027, it could reach 100.

The reason is simple: data centers need three things in abundance—power, land, and water. The Great Lakes region has all three, plus something else: a climate that provides free cooling for half the year.

The Environmental Equation

The numbers seem staggering—billions of gallons of water. But context matters. The conservative estimate of 15 billion gallons represents what Chicago's residents use in just 40 days.

The real concern isn't quantity—it's temperature. Water discharged at 95°F can create thermal plumes that affect fish spawning grounds and alter local ecosystems. Three facilities have already been cited for violations.

What Comes Next

Michigan just passed legislation requiring environmental impact studies for facilities using over 5 million gallons annually. Wisconsin is considering similar measures. Ontario has implemented the strictest rules yet: mandatory closed-loop cooling for any facility within 10 miles of the lakes.

The data center boom isn't slowing—if anything, AI's hunger for computing power is accelerating it. The question isn't whether the Great Lakes region will become America's data hub. It's whether we'll manage that transformation wisely.
